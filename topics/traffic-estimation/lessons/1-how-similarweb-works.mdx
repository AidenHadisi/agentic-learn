# How SimilarWeb Actually Works

<div className="lesson-meta">Understand the methodology behind traffic estimation tools so you know what to replicate — and what to skip.</div>

SimilarWeb, Semrush, and Ahrefs all claim to tell you how much traffic any website gets. But they use fundamentally different approaches, and none of them are measuring — they're **estimating**. Understanding how they work (and fail) is the foundation for building your own.

## The Two Approaches

There are two distinct methodologies in the market, and confusing them is one of the most common mistakes:

<Tabs>
	<Tab label="Panel/Clickstream (SimilarWeb, Semrush Trends)">
		**How it works:** Observe real browsing behavior from a panel of opted-in users (browser extensions, SDKs, ISP partnerships), then extrapolate to the full population.

		**What it estimates:** Total traffic across all channels — direct, organic, paid, social, referral.

		**Analogy:** Nielsen TV ratings. A sample of households have meters; ratings for the whole country are extrapolated from that sample.
	</Tab>
	<Tab label="Search Model (Ahrefs, Semrush Domain Analytics)">
		**How it works:** Crawl SERPs, find every keyword a site ranks for, multiply rank-position CTR by search volume, and sum.

		**What it estimates:** Organic search traffic only — not total visits.

		**Analogy:** Estimating a store's foot traffic by counting how many people see its billboard ads and applying an average "walk-in rate."
	</Tab>
</Tabs>

<Callout variant="key">
SimilarWeb reports **total visits** from a panel. Ahrefs reports **estimated organic clicks** from a keyword model. These are completely different numbers measuring different things. Never compare them directly.
</Callout>

## Panel-Based Estimation: The Core Method

SimilarWeb's approach (and the one we'll replicate) is panel-based estimation. Here's how it works at a high level:

<Stepper>
	<Step>**Collect** browsing data from millions of opted-in devices — browser extensions, mobile SDKs, and partnership feeds from ISPs and measurement firms.</Step>
	<Step>**Clean** the data — strip PII, filter bots, normalize across devices and geographies, remove anomalies.</Step>
	<Step>**Classify** each page visit by traffic source (organic, direct, social, etc.) using referrer signals and URL patterns.</Step>
	<Step>**Extrapolate** panel observations to population-level estimates using statistical weighting by country, device type, demographics, and industry.</Step>
	<Step>**Calibrate** against ground truth — millions of sites share their actual analytics (like GA4), which the model uses as training labels.</Step>
</Stepper>

### Panel sizes in the industry

| Tool | Panel claim | Notes |
|------|------------|-------|
| SimilarWeb | "Millions of devices," 10B signals/day | Never publishes an exact panelist count |
| Semrush Trends | 200M+ users, 190+ countries | Most explicit public claim; sources from hundreds of clickstream providers |
| Jumpshot (defunct) | ~100M devices, 5B actions/day | Shut down Jan 2020 after privacy scandal |

### The Jumpshot cautionary tale

Jumpshot was the gold standard clickstream provider until 2020. It was powered by Avast antivirus — installed on ~435M devices — which quietly logged browsing data from users who thought they were getting free virus protection. When journalists exposed the re-identification risk, Avast shut Jumpshot overnight. ~250 jobs lost, $180M in value destroyed.

This matters for two reasons:

1. **Much of the "panel truth" in SEO research from 2016-2019 was Jumpshot data.** Zero-click studies, Facebook traffic analyses, etc. — that underlying data source no longer exists.
2. **It proves panel composition matters enormously.** Jumpshot's panel was "people who install free antivirus" — a specific demographic, not a random sample of internet users.

<Callout variant="warn">
Traffic estimates are only as good as **who is in the panel**. Jumpshot skewed toward desktop users who install free software. SimilarWeb skews toward users who install browser extensions. Neither is a random sample of the internet.
</Callout>

## SimilarWeb's Four Data Sources

SimilarWeb combines four distinct data sources (they call it the "Dataverse"):

<Mermaid chart={`flowchart TD
	A["1. Direct Measurement<br/>Sites sharing their GA/analytics"] --> E[ML Pipeline]
	B["2. Contributory Network<br/>SimilarWeb's own extensions/SDKs"] --> E
	C["3. Partnerships<br/>ISP, DSP, measurement firms"] --> E
	D["4. Public Data<br/>Crawled web, census, app stores"] --> E
	E --> F["Cleaning & Classification"]
	F --> G["Statistical Weighting & Calibration"]
	G --> H["Traffic Estimates"]
`} />

| Source | What it provides | Role in the model |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Direct Measurement** | Real GA4/analytics from millions of sites that opt in | Ground truth labels for training |
| **Contributory Network** | Anonymized browsing from SimilarWeb's own products | Core clickstream panel |
| **Partnerships** | Pre-analyzed signals from ISPs, DSPs, measurement firms | Additional behavioral data |
| **Public Data** | Crawled web pages, census/population data, app store listings | Features and priors for scaling |

<Callout variant="tip">
The "Direct Measurement" source is the most important for accuracy. Sites that share their real analytics with SimilarWeb get better estimates for themselves **and** their competitors in the same niche — because the model has ground truth to calibrate against.
</Callout>

### What this means for your DIY tool

You already have an analog of source #1 (labeled data from sites you track). Sources #2 and #3 are the expensive parts — you'd need to build or buy clickstream. Source #4 (public data) is free and where we'll get most of our features.

The key insight: **you don't need a panel to estimate traffic**. You need:
- Labels (ground truth visits for a training set)
- Features (public signals that correlate with traffic)
- A model that maps features → visits

SimilarWeb has a huge panel that improves accuracy, but even they calibrate against first-party analytics. If you have good labels, you can train a model on public features alone.

## How Channel Classification Works

SimilarWeb breaks traffic into channels. Here's what signals they actually use:

| Channel | Primary signal | Classification method |
|---------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Direct** | No HTTP referrer | Absence of referrer = typed URL, bookmark, or "dark traffic" |
| **Organic Search** | Referrer is a search engine domain | google.com, bing.com, etc. without paid URL markers |
| **Paid Search** | Search engine referrer + ad markers | `gclid` param, `/aclk` paths, sponsored click patterns |
| **Social (Organic)** | Social media domain as referrer | facebook.com, reddit.com, twitter.com, etc. |
| **Social (Paid)** | Social referrer + click IDs/UTMs | `fbclid`, `utm_medium=paid_social`, etc. |
| **Referral** | Other website as referrer | Any domain not classified as search/social |
| **Display** | Ad network landing patterns | Programmatic ad host referrers |
| **Email** | Webmail domain as referrer | gmail.com, outlook.com web versions only |
| **Gen AI** | AI chatbot domain as referrer | chatgpt.com, gemini.google.com, perplexity.ai |

### The "dark traffic" problem

This is the single biggest source of misclassification — and it affects every tool including GA4:

<Reveal prompt="What everyday actions produce 'dark traffic' that gets mislabeled as Direct?">
- **Messaging apps** (WhatsApp, Slack, iMessage, Telegram) — strip or omit the Referer header
- **Native email apps** (Outlook desktop, Apple Mail, Gmail app) — no web referrer
- **QR codes** without UTM parameters
- **Links in PDFs, Word docs, presentations**
- **Privacy browsers and extensions** that block referrers
- **HTTPS → HTTP transitions** where browsers strip the referrer

All of these show up as "Direct" traffic even though the user clicked a link from somewhere specific. Industry estimates suggest 10-20% of "Direct" traffic is actually from identifiable sources.
</Reveal>

<Callout variant="key">
For your DIY tool, channel classification from public signals alone is the **hardest** problem. Without seeing actual referrer data in a clickstream, you can only estimate organic search (via keyword rankings) and infer rough channel proportions from industry benchmarks. We'll tackle this in Section 5.
</Callout>

## How Geo Distribution Works

SimilarWeb determines country-level traffic splits using:

1. **Panel observation location** — each clickstream event has a device/network location (not IP-based; SimilarWeb explicitly says they don't collect IPs)
2. **Census/population priors** — public demographic data helps scale panel observations to national populations
3. **Country-specific models** — localized ML models for major markets (US, UK, BR, IN, JP, RU)

Important nuances:
- Geo = where the **user** is, not where the URL is targeted. Visiting `sephora.com.au` doesn't auto-assign you to Australia.
- Panels are denser in US/EU where extension adoption is highest. Emerging markets are noisier.
- Only ~58 countries get full independent geo breakdown. Others fold into "Worldwide."

## How Accurate Are These Tools?

This is the most important section. Three major studies give us empirical accuracy numbers:

### SparkToro study (641 sites)

- Best correlation: Semrush at 0.79, Datos at 0.72, SimilarWeb at 0.66
- Ahrefs: 0.50 — but it only measures organic, so this isn't a fair comparison
- **Within ±30% of GA:** ~63% of estimates for large sites (250k+); much worse for small sites
- Error bars **often exceed ±100%** — even for the best tools
- SimilarWeb was strongest for 5k–100k monthly users, worst on sites under 5k

### Ahrefs self-study (1,635 sites vs GSC)

- Median deviation from Google Search Console: **49.5%**
- Pearson correlation: **0.76**
- Semrush on the same sites: 68% median deviation, 0.74 correlation
- Range per-site: from under 5% to over 1,000% off

### Academic study (86 sites, Jansen et al. 2022)

- SimilarWeb underestimated total visits by **19%** on aggregate
- Underestimated unique visitors by **39%**
- But ranking correlation was **0.954** — the relative ordering is very reliable

<Callout variant="key" title="The accuracy lesson">
These tools are **directional compasses, not speedometers**. They tell you who's bigger than whom (rank correlation 0.75–0.95) but routinely miss absolute visit counts by 20-50%+. For your DIY tool, optimize for **rank accuracy within a peer set**, not exact visit numbers.
</Callout>

### What accuracy should you aim for?

Based on the published research, here are realistic targets for a DIY tool:

| Metric | Realistic target | Industry best |
|--------|-----------------|---------------|
| Rank correlation (Spearman) within a niche | ≥0.75 | 0.79–0.95 |
| Median absolute % error (mid-size sites) | 30–50% | ~20% |
| % of estimates within ±30% | 40–55% | ~63% |
| Small sites (under 5k visits) | Directional only or refuse to estimate | SimilarWeb hides these |

### The metric mismatch trap

One subtle but critical point: **GA Users ≠ Visits ≠ Sessions ≠ Unique Visitors.**

## Putting It Together: What to Replicate

	items={[

**What we're keeping from SimilarWeb's approach:**
- Panel-calibrated estimation (we have labels)
- Multi-source feature fusion (public data)
- Per-niche calibration
- Confidence intervals and minimum-traffic thresholds

**What we're skipping:**
- Building/buying a clickstream panel ($$$)
- ISP/CDN partnerships ($$$)
- Browser extensions (privacy nightmare, Jumpshot lesson)
