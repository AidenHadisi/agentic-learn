# Descriptive Statistics

<div className="lesson-meta">Summarize data with the right tools — and connect them to ML</div>

## Why This Matters

Every data science project starts with the same question: *what does this data look like?* Descriptive statistics are your first-pass answer. But they're more than EDA — the choice of summary statistic is deeply connected to the loss functions that drive machine learning models.

## Measures of Center

You know mean, median, and mode. But do you know *which one your model is optimizing*?

<Callout variant="key">
**MSE minimizes at the mean.** If you train a model with mean squared error, its prediction for a group converges to the group's mean. **MAE minimizes at the median.** Switch to mean absolute error and your model targets the median instead.[¹]
</Callout>

This isn't trivia — it's a design choice. When your target variable is right-skewed (revenue, latency, house prices), the mean gets pulled toward outliers. A model trained with MSE will overpredict for typical cases while chasing the tail.

### When to use what

| Statistic | Use when | Fragile to |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Mean | Symmetric data, or you specifically want to account for magnitude | Outliers, skew |
| Median | Skewed data, ordinal scales, or you want a "typical" value | Nothing — it's robust |
| Mode | Categorical data, or multimodal distributions | Small samples |

**Real example:** US household income is right-skewed. The mean (~$105k) is pulled up by high earners; the median (~$75k) better represents a "typical" household.[²] If you're building a model to predict "what income should we expect for a new user," your loss function choice determines which of these you're targeting.

## Measures of Spread

### Variance and Standard Deviation

Variance measures the average squared deviation from the mean:

$$\sigma^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^2$$

Standard deviation is its square root — back in the original units.

<Callout variant="warn">
**Sample vs population:** use \(n-1\) (Bessel's correction) for sample variance. With large datasets this barely matters, but it's the default in `numpy`'s `ddof=1` and pandas. Getting this wrong won't ruin your analysis, but know why the distinction exists — it makes the sample variance an unbiased estimator of the population variance.
</Callout>

### Intuition for SD

One SD from the mean captures "the typical spread." For roughly symmetric data:
- ~68% within 1 SD (for Normal data exactly)
- ~95% within 2 SD
- **Chebyshev's guarantee (any distribution):** at least 75% within 2 SD, at least 89% within 3 SD[³]

### IQR — The Robust Alternative

The interquartile range (Q3 − Q1) measures spread of the middle 50%. It ignores tails entirely, making it immune to outliers.

**DS application:** When monitoring model performance metrics (latency, prediction error), IQR-based outlier detection (values beyond 1.5×IQR from quartiles) is far more stable than mean ± 2σ on skewed distributions.

## Shape: Skewness and Kurtosis

### Skewness

Measures asymmetry. Right-skewed (positive) means a long right tail.

**Common right-skewed data in DS:** revenue per user, page load time, ad click-through rates, word frequencies, file sizes.

**The fix:** log-transform right-skewed features before feeding to models that assume normality (linear regression residuals, distance-based methods). But always check — the transform should make sense for the domain.

### Kurtosis

Measures **tail heaviness**, not "peakedness" (a widespread misconception[⁴]). High kurtosis means more probability mass in the tails — more extreme outliers than a Normal distribution would predict.

**DS relevance:** Financial returns have high kurtosis (fat tails). If you assume Normal tails when modeling risk, you'll dramatically underestimate the probability of crashes.

## The DS Connection: Feature Engineering

<Stepper>
	<Step>**Compute descriptives** — mean, SD, skewness for each feature. High skew? Consider log-transform.</Step>
	<Step>**Check for outliers** — IQR method or domain knowledge. Decide: clip, transform, or use robust methods.</Step>
	<Step>**Scale features** — Standard scaling (subtract mean, divide by SD) makes gradient descent behave better. But if outliers are present, use RobustScaler (subtract median, divide by IQR).</Step>
	<Step>**Choose your model's loss** — Right-skewed target? MAE or Huber loss might be more appropriate than MSE.</Step>
</Stepper>

## Practice

Think about each scenario before revealing the answer:

**Scenario 1:** You're reporting "typical session duration" on a website. Most sessions are 1–5 minutes, but some users leave tabs open for hours.

<Reveal prompt="Which summary and why?">
**Median** (or median + IQR). The long-tail sessions inflate the mean dramatically, misrepresenting the typical user experience. You might also report the 90th percentile to characterize power users.
</Reveal>

**Scenario 2:** You're computing the "average revenue per user" to estimate total revenue if you acquire 10,000 new users.

<Reveal prompt="Which summary and why?">
**Mean** — and here the mean is exactly right! Total revenue = mean × count. The median would underestimate total revenue for right-skewed distributions. This is why mean matters for business totals even when median is better for "typical."
</Reveal>

**Scenario 3:** Your model's residuals have a standard deviation of 15 but a kurtosis of 8 (much higher than Normal's 3). What should concern you?

<Reveal prompt="What's the concern?">
High kurtosis means your residuals have fatter tails than Normal. The "2σ captures 95%" rule understates how often your model makes large errors. Your confidence intervals are too narrow. Consider: robust regression, Huber loss, or acknowledging wider prediction intervals.
</Reveal>

## Key Takeaways

<Callout variant="key">
1. **Mean vs median is a loss function choice** — MSE → mean, MAE → median
2. **SD is fragile** — one extreme outlier can dominate it; use IQR for robust spread
3. **Skewness tells you whether to transform** — right-skewed features often benefit from log-transform
4. **Kurtosis is about tails, not peaks** — high kurtosis means your Normal-based confidence intervals are too narrow
</Callout>
